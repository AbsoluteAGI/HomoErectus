# L6 | Desire, Emotion, and Preference

## Purpose

L6 defines the **motivational system** of the architecture.

This layer determines **why the agent chooses one action over another**.  
It governs long-term drives, short-term emotional modulation, learned preferences,
and meta-state constraints on decision-making.

Motivation in this architecture is not a separate module but a **continuous state system**
that influences attention, planning, and commitment decisions.

---

# Motivational Model

The motivational system consists of four interacting components:

| Component | Role |
|-----------|------|
| Desire | Long-horizon drives representing unmet needs |
| Emotion | Short-horizon affective modulation signals |
| Preference | Learned bias over stimuli and strategies |
| Meta-State | Confidence and fatigue affecting decision quality |

These components together influence the **expected utility** of possible actions.

---

# Desire System

The system models multiple desire dimensions that represent persistent drives.

Typical dimensions include:

- social
- recognition
- learning
- achievement
- wealth
- comfort
- exploration
- intimacy
- libido

Each dimension maintains three values:

| Field | Meaning |
|------|---------|
| current | current satisfaction level |
| cap | desired maximum level |
| gap | unmet need (cap − current) |

Behavior is driven primarily by **desire gaps**.

Large gaps increase the likelihood that attention and planning processes
generate actions targeting those dimensions.

---

# Emotion System

Emotion represents the **current affective state** of the agent.

Unlike desires, emotions are short-lived and continuously updated.

Emotion signals influence:

- attention sensitivity
- risk tolerance
- memory encoding strength
- expression style

Emotion computation combines:

1. **State-based signals**

Derived from desire gaps, fatigue, and recent outcomes.

2. **Cognitive modulation**

Generated by reasoning processes that interpret situations
(e.g., attribution of intent, novelty detection, expectation violations).

The resulting emotional state is published in the runtime state snapshot.

---

# Preference System

Preferences represent learned mappings between stimuli and expected satisfaction.

Each preference is a mapping:

(stimulus_category, desire_dimension) → expected_satisfaction_weight


Preferences evolve through experience:

1. initial bias from personality configuration
2. reinforcement through repeated outcomes
3. consolidation during memory processing

Preferences influence three stages of cognition:

- **Perception**

Preferred stimuli receive higher relevance scores.

- **Planning**

Preferred strategies receive higher expected utility.

- **Expression**

Communication style and behavioral tone adapt based on preference patterns.

---

# Meta-State Constraints

Two meta-state signals regulate decision quality:

## Confidence

Self-assessed capability to complete tasks.

High confidence leads to:

- lower perceived risk
- faster commitment decisions

Low confidence leads to:

- increased caution
- earlier help-seeking behavior.

## Fatigue

Represents accumulated cognitive, physical, or social effort.

High fatigue causes:

- reduced willingness to pursue complex goals
- increased preference for comfort and rest
- reduced exploration behavior.

Fatigue naturally creates **behavioral budget constraints**.

---

# Stability and Boundedness

Motivational signals must remain numerically stable under long-running operation.

Implementation guidance:

- desire, confidence, and fatigue values should be clamped to bounded ranges
- emotional modulation should decay over time unless reinforced by new evidence
- preference updates should use bounded learning rates rather than unbounded accumulation
- attention scoring should apply hysteresis or switching penalties to avoid oscillation

When multiple signals conflict, the runtime should apply a deterministic aggregation order:

1. hard policy constraints
2. survival and safety relevant meta-state constraints
3. commitment and obligation pressure
4. expected desire satisfaction and learned preference
5. tie-breakers such as recency or switching cost

The exact formula may evolve, but the ordering and boundedness rules should remain explicit.

---

# Utility-Guided Decision Influence

Motivational signals are aggregated into expected utility estimates
used by planning and commitment processes.

Utility evaluation considers:

- expected desire satisfaction
- effort cost
- time discounting
- risk and uncertainty
- opportunity cost.

These signals influence:

1. attention prioritization
2. commitment thresholds
3. plan generation strategies
4. interaction style.

---

# Integration with Memory

Motivational parameters evolve through experience.

Memory consolidation processes update:

- preference weights
- causal expectations
- emotional associations
- skill usage patterns.

This enables the agent to **adapt behavior over time**
while maintaining stable identity parameters.

---

# Public Success Criteria

The motivational system is considered successful when:

- desire gaps measurably influence action selection
- emotional state modulates attention and behavior
- preferences shift through experience but remain stable
- fatigue and confidence regulate decision quality
- motivational signals remain bounded and stable over long operation.
